Fetching Objects for Residents

Residents need assistance retrieving things, because they often have mobility or perceptual impairments.

The robot can respond to basic requests to help residents locate objects and/or fetch things for them. This action can be passive (i.e. resident makes request to robot) or pro-active (i.e. robot suggests that it fetches something for the resident).

Temporary Relief for Carers

Care staff need additional support throughout the day because they have a demanding job and can only do one thing at a time.

In pressurised situations, a carer can summon the robot and ask it to perform a task that would help free up the carers time.

2/20

Basic Information Retrieval

Residents need new ways to access news and information because often technology is inaccessible to them.

Residents can simply ask the robot a question that they want the answer to. The robot can connect to a service like Siri or Alexa, and in turn can deliver a suitable response.

3/20

Navigation Guidance for People with Dementia

Residents with dementia or MCI need assistance getting to places because they are prone to getting confused and lost without help.

The robot can escort residents to locations where their presence is presently needed, or to a destination that the resident requests.

4/20

Provide regular updates to carers

Care organisations need to ensure staff are constantly up-to-date on the health status of individual residents because lack of awareness places vulnerable residents at high risk.

The robot can provide updates to staff as they start their shifts. The robot can also perform spot checks to ensure staff are aware of current issues. This spot-check could be implemented on an opt-in basis, where there is an incentive for achieving high scores.

5/20

Providing reminders to residents

Care organisations need to remind residents about events (mealtimes, therapy sessions, etc.) because many residents have memory problems.

The robot can locate residents in a building and let them know that their presence will soon be required. If necessary, the robot can escort them to the appropriate location.

6/20

Helping with menu choice

Care organisations need to frequently determine the dietary preferences of residents because food menus change on a daily basis.

The robot can navigate to each resident, and ask take their food order as appropriate. The robot can use its screens to show images of the meal, as well as provide a verbal and/or an accompanying text description.

7/20

Measure Environmental Conditions

Care organisations need to continuously monitor conditions in each facility because changes to the environment can pose a significant risk to residents.

The robot possesses a range of sensors that can detect environmental conditions at a given point in time. By moving around, the robot can build a detailed map of the environmental conditions throughout the day.

8/20

Information Provision to Individual Residents

Residents need access to information that is personal to them because they should maintain their independence to the greatest extent possible.

Care staff can provide the robot with access to personal information for each resident. The robot can relay this information to the resident when requested.

9/20

Remote presence of management

Management of care organisations need to supervise care workers because they are accountable for everything that happens in the facility.

Using an immersive control interface, the robot can be tele-operated by managers of the organisation. This functionality may be used to enable managers to perform remote checks, or provide a presence during/after an incident has occurred.

10/20

Mediator for family to contact resident

LTC organisations need to facilitate remote communication between residents and family because many older people can’t/don’t use mobile phones.

The robot possesses the means to facilitate tele-conferencing (i.e. like Skype). Family of residents can communicate with residents directly using an app, without needing to call the main desk of the facility.

11/20

Taking attendance during group therapy

Care workers need to record/log resident data (while also performing care related functions) because such information is useful in assessing the changing health status of residents.

The robot can monitor and record when/if a resident has attended a therapy session. Where appropriate, the robot can share this information with the family of the resident (i.e. send them a photo of the resident engaging in the activity).

12/20

Keeping residents company in communal areas

Residents need to engage in social interaction because they often get bored and loneliness constitutes a major health risk.

The robot can mediate social interaction between the resident and a remote tele-operator (who can control the robot and has access to the robot’s camera feed). This tele-operator could be a family member, member of a befriending service or a medical professional.

13/20

Providing gold standard transparency

Organisations need to demonstrate quality of care because damaging accusations are often made and care workers can get unfairly blamed when something bad happens on a shift.

The robot can record (audio and visual) what it sees and hears during normal operation. In the event of an incident (i.e. claim that a care worker did something wrong), data collected from the robot can be used to provide clarity.

14/20

Short-term monitoring of residents

Care organisations often need to closely monitor certain residents (i.e. after medication change) because they may be susceptible to significant changes in behaviour, energy levels, etc.

The robot can observe and monitor (audio and visual) the behaviour of a resident over a period of time. This data can be assessed, either in real-time or retrospectively, by a qualified care professional.

15/20

Performing Health and Safety Assessments

Care organisations need to closely monitor the environment because they are required to abide by health and safety requirements.

The robot can perform building audits and take measures to ensure the facility is compliant with H&S regulations. If the robot determines that the facility is not being adherent with a regulation, an alert can be raised to inform a staff member, who can then review the situation.

16/20

Greeter in the lobby/reception

Care organisations need to regulate how their facilities are accessed their because they are responsible for the well-being of vulnerable members of society.

The robot can greet visitors, family of residents, etc. as they enter the facility. It can perform security and receptionist functions, as well be able to lead visitors to the current location of a resident.

17/20

Bedside observation

Care organisations need to closely monitor some residents because they pose a high risk of hurting themselves (i.e. falling) if left unsupervised for long periods of time.

The robot can locate itself beside a resident for prolonged durations of time. If the robot detects that the resident is about to get up, an alert can be sent to a remote tele-operator who can assume control of the robot and take appropriate measures.

18/20

Rapid response to alarms

Care organisations need to rapidly respond to alarms because they provide explicit indication that help is needed.

The robot can respond immediately when a resident triggers a personal alarm, or an environment sensor detects a possible problem. Once the robot locates the resident, a tele-operator assumes control of the robot who can assess the situation and respond accordingly.

19/20

Perform security patrols

Care organisations need to ensure their facilities are secure, especially at night, because they are responsible for the safety and well-being of their residents.

The robot can patrol the facility to ensure that security measures have been taken (closed windows, doors, etc.). If the security has been breached, the robot can raise an alarm and alert a tele-operator who can take over control of the robot.
